## Abstract

A Chosen-Plaintext Attack (CPA) is a cryptographic analysis game for encryption, where an adversary queries an encryption oracle with plaintexts and observes the mapping to their ciphertexts. 
At an arbitrary time, it provides two challenge plaintexts but receives only one ciphertext, and finally guesses which of the two challenge plaintexts has been encrypted. 
Neural distinguishers, as a powerful representative of Artificial Intelligence (AI) methods, have been recently used in cryptographic analysis methods. 
However, they cannot directly be applied to perform CPA due to different input requirements and objectives. 
This work aims to address this gap. We provide the first rigorous and systematic formulation of CPA from a deep learning perspective.
Specifically, we introduce **NeuralCPA**, a novel deep neural network-based method designed for the evaluation of block cipher CPA security as an initial effort for AI-based CPA analysis. 
We empirically validate its effectiveness across a diverse range of block ciphers, including SIMON, SPECK, LEA, HIGHT, XTEA, TEA, PRESENT, AES, and KATAN. 
Our experimental results confirm that NeuralCPA consistently achieves significant distinguishing advantages in round-reduced settings. Notably, our attack success rate ranges from 51% to 76.4%.

</HighlightedSection>

## Contributions

- **Deep learning formulation of CPA.** We present the first rigorous and systematic formulation of CPA from a deep learning perspective, casting the CPA experiment as a supervised learning task.
- **Analysis for block ciphers.** We introduce NeuralCPA, a neural network–based framework for evaluating the CPA security of block ciphers and empirically demonstrate its effectiveness.
- **Extensions for multi-instance settings.** We show that NeuralCPA can be extended to multi-instance settings, where even a marginal advantage can be ideally amplified.

## Overview

<strong>Problem.</strong><br />
In the CPA game, two plaintexts $m_0$ and $m_1$ are given. A hidden bit $b \in \{0,1\}$ selects one of them, and $m_b$ is encrypted by $E_k$ to produce the challenge ciphertext $c^\star$. The goal is to guess $b$.

<strong>Idea.</strong><br />
NeuralCPA reformulates this problem as a supervised learning task by leveraging the distinguishing capability of neural networks.

The overall workflow of NeuralCPA is shown below.<br />

<strong>Training.</strong><br />

1\. Sample positive pairs $\mathcal{P}^+$ satisfying the preset input difference $\delta$ (e.g., pairs of the form $(m, m \oplus \delta)$ for a plaintext $m$), and negative pairs $\mathcal{P}^-$ not satisfying $\delta$, which are merged into the set $\mathcal{P}$.<br />
2\. Encrypt all pairs in $\mathcal{P}$ using the block cipher $E_k$ to obtain the ciphertext pairs $\mathcal{C}$.<br />
3\. Train a neural distinguisher on $\mathcal{C}$ to predict whether the corresponding plaintext pair satisfies $\delta$.<br />

<strong>Testing.</strong><br />
<div class="scale-90 origin-center mx-auto">
  <Figure>
    <Picture
      slot="figure"
      src="../assets/post-phase.pdf"
      alt=""

1\. Construct auxiliary plaintexts as $m_{i,aux} = m_i \oplus \delta$, $i \in \{0,1\}$.<br />
2\. Encrypt the auxiliary plaintexts using $E_k$ to obtain $c_{i,aux}$.<br />
3\. Use the trained distinguisher to compute a score $p_i$ for each ciphertext pair $(c^\star, c_{i,aux})$.<br />
4\. Output the final guess $i = \arg\max_i p_i$.<br />

<strong>Intuition.</strong><br />
The neural distinguisher is trained to distinguish ciphertext pairs whose underlying plaintext pairs follow the difference $\delta$.
If $c^\star = E_k(m_b)$, then $(c^\star, c_{b,aux})$ forms a differential pair $(E_k(m_b), E_k(m_b \oplus \delta))$, which the distinguisher is more likely to recognize. 
Consequently, the score $p_b$ tends to be larger than $p_{1-b}$, enabling recovery of $b$ by selecting $i = \arg\max_i p_i$.

## Methodology

When mapped to the standard CPA experiment, NeuralCPA operates in three stages:<br />
(i) *Training the neural distinguisher* during the pre-challenge phase.<br />
(ii) *Construct auxiliary ciphertext pairs using the input difference* in the post-challenge phase.<br />
(iii) *Choose the plaintext by comparing auxiliary scores* in the guess phase.
